My Complete 3D Printing Setup & What I Recommend

Updated March 2026 · 10 min read · By Layered Up

People always ask me: "What printer do you use? What filament? What tools do you need?" So I figured it was time to lay it all out. This is my exact 3D printing setup — everything from the printer itself to the little accessories that make daily printing smoother. Whether you're building your first setup or upgrading, this is what I'd buy if I were starting from scratch today.

🖨️ The Printer: Bambu Lab A1

Bambu Lab A1

This is the heart of the operation. The Bambu Lab A1 changed the game for me — it's fast, it's reliable, and it "just works" in a way that most 3D printers don't. Coming from an Ender 3, the difference was night and day. Auto bed leveling, auto calibration, and speeds that make FDM feel almost as convenient as a regular paper printer.

Why I chose it: Speed (up to 500mm/s), auto everything, Bambu Studio software is excellent, and the AMS compatibility means multi-color prints are on the table.

What I'd change: Honestly, not much. The open frame means you'll want an enclosure if you're printing ABS or ASA, but for PLA and PETG it's perfect as-is.

🎨 Filament: What I Keep on the Shelf

I always have at least 5-6 spools ready to go. Here's my regular rotation:

Daily Drivers

  • Bambu Lab PLA Basic — My go-to for anything that needs to look clean. The consistency is unmatched, and it works perfectly with the A1's auto settings.
  • Hatchbox PLA — The reliable workhorse. I grab this when I'm prototyping or doing prints where I need a specific color Bambu doesn't offer.
  • eSUN PLA+ — For anything functional. Phone holders, clips, brackets — this stuff is genuinely tough.

Specialty Filaments

  • SUNLU Silk PLA — When I want that premium metallic look. Great for gifts and display pieces.
  • Mika3D Rainbow Silk PLA — The TikTok crowd-pleaser. Every vase print with this stuff goes viral.
  • SUNLU TPU — For flexible prints like phone cases and grip accessories.
  • Overture PETG — When I need heat resistance or outdoor durability.

🔧 Essential Tools

These are the things you don't think about until you need them — and then you really need them.

Print Removal & Cleanup

Getting prints off the bed cleanly is an art. These tools make it foolproof:

  • 3D Print Removal Tool — A proper metal scraper with a thin, flexible blade. Don't use a kitchen spatula. Trust me.
  • Micro Scraper Set — For cleaning up small details, removing support material, and deburring edges.

Bed Adhesion

The Bambu A1's textured PEI plate is already great, but for tricky filaments (PETG, large PLA prints), I keep Magigoo on hand. One thin coat and your print sticks perfectly — then pops right off when the bed cools.

🛒 Magigoo on Amazon

Filament Storage

Moisture ruins filament. Period. I store all my open spools in a SUNLU dry box, and sealed spools go in vacuum bags with desiccant. This single habit has eliminated 90% of my print quality issues.

  • SUNLU Filament Dry Box — Feed filament directly from the box to your printer. Keeps it dry while you print.
  • Desiccant Packs — Throw these in every storage container. Recharge them in the oven monthly.
  • Vacuum Storage Bags — For long-term spool storage. Seal it up with a desiccant pack and forget about it.

💡 Tips for Building Your Setup

  1. Start with a good printer, not a cheap one. The money you save on a $150 printer, you'll spend in frustration and upgrades. The Bambu A1 or A1 Mini are the best starting points in 2026.
  2. Buy 2-3 colors of reliable PLA first. Don't hoard 20 spools of exotic filament. Get comfortable with the basics.
  3. Invest in storage immediately. Don't wait until your filament goes bad. A dry box and some desiccant will save you money in the long run.
  4. Get proper tools. A $10 scraper set pays for itself on the first stubborn print.
  5. Join the community. Reddit's r/3Dprinting, r/BambuLab, and the Bambu wiki are goldmines for troubleshooting.

Total Setup Cost Breakdown

Here's roughly what you'd spend to replicate my entire setup:

  • Bambu Lab A1: ~$400
  • 3-4 spools of filament: ~$80
  • Tools (scraper, removal tool): ~$25
  • Storage (dry box + desiccant): ~$55
  • Bed adhesive (Magigoo): ~$15

Total: ~$575 for a setup that can produce professional-quality prints consistently. That's less than most people spend on a gaming console — and this one makes you money.

Final Thoughts

Building the right setup takes some planning, but once you have it dialed in, 3D printing becomes a genuinely productive hobby (or business). Don't overthink it — grab a solid printer, reliable filament, and basic tools, and start printing. You'll learn what you need as you go.
