How to Store 3D Printer Filament: Complete Guide

Updated March 2026 · 7 min read · By Layered Up

Here's a fact that ruins a lot of beginners' prints: 3D printer filament absorbs moisture from the air. And wet filament prints terribly — popping sounds during extrusion, rough surface finish, stringing everywhere, and weakened layer adhesion. The good news? Proper storage is easy, cheap, and will save you from throwing away expensive spools. Here's everything you need to know.

Why Moisture Ruins Filament

Most 3D printing filaments are hygroscopic — they absorb water vapor from the air. When you push wet filament through a hot nozzle (200°C+), that trapped moisture instantly turns to steam. The steam creates tiny bubbles in the extruded plastic, which causes:

  • Popping/crackling sounds during printing
  • Rough, bubbly surface texture
  • Excessive stringing between print features
  • Weak layer bonds — prints snap easily
  • Inconsistent extrusion — under-extrusion followed by blobs
  • Warping and poor bed adhesion
⚠️ How Fast Does It Happen? In a humid environment (60%+ RH), PLA can absorb enough moisture to affect print quality in as little as 24-48 hours. TPU and Nylon can be affected in just a few hours. Don't leave your filament out.

If you're printing with TPU or specialty silk PLA, proper storage isn't optional — it's mandatory for usable prints.

Storage Method 2: Vacuum Bags (Best for Stored Spools)

Vacuum Storage Bags + Desiccant

For spools you're not currently using, vacuum bags are the most cost-effective long-term solution. Remove the air, throw in a desiccant pack, and your filament stays factory-fresh for months.

Our method:

  1. Put the spool in a vacuum bag
  2. Add 1-2 desiccant packs
  3. Squeeze out as much air as possible and seal
  4. Store in a cool, dry place away from sunlight

We've stored Overture PLA this way for 6+ months and it printed perfectly when we opened it.

Storage Method 3: Airtight Containers (Budget DIY)

If you don't want to buy a dedicated dry box, large airtight containers (like Sterilite gasket bins or cereal containers) work surprisingly well. Here's the setup:

  1. Get a container that fits 2-4 spools
  2. Line the bottom with desiccant packs
  3. Stack your spools inside
  4. Seal the container — the gasket keeps air (and moisture) out
  5. Add a cheap hygrometer to monitor humidity (optional but helpful)

This method costs maybe $15-20 total and stores multiple spools. It won't actively dry wet filament like the SUNLU box, but it prevents moisture absorption effectively.

How to Tell If Your Filament Is Wet

Not sure if your filament has absorbed moisture? Here's what to look for:

  • Listen: Popping or crackling sounds during extrusion = wet filament
  • Look: Surface is rough, bubbly, or has a matte texture when it should be smooth
  • Feel: The filament snaps more easily than usual (especially PLA)
  • Print quality: Excessive stringing, zits on the surface, poor layer adhesion
💡 Quick Test: Extrude some filament into thin air (no print, just push it through). Dry filament comes out smooth and consistent. Wet filament sputters, pops, and produces a rough, foamy strand.

How to Dry Wet Filament

Already have a wet spool? Here's how to save it:

Option 1: Filament Dry Box

The SUNLU dry box can double as a dryer. Set the temperature and let it run:

  • PLA: 45°C for 4-6 hours
  • PETG: 65°C for 4-6 hours
  • TPU: 55°C for 6-8 hours

Option 2: Food Dehydrator

A food dehydrator with adjustable temperature works great. Set it to the temps above and place the spool inside. Make sure the temp doesn't exceed the filament's glass transition temperature.

Option 3: Oven (Use Caution)

You can use a regular oven, but be very careful — most oven thermostats aren't accurate at low temperatures, and overshooting can warp or fuse the spool. If you go this route, use an oven thermometer to verify the actual temperature.

⚠️ Never dry filament at a temperature above its glass transition temp. PLA softens around 60°C — if your oven runs hot, you'll have a melted blob instead of a spool.

Our Storage System

Here's exactly how we manage our filament inventory:

Proper filament storage is the single biggest quality-of-life improvement you can make to your 3D printing setup. It's not glamorous, it's not exciting, but it eliminates so many frustrating print failures that it's absolutely worth doing right from the start.
